That Voice Is Yours

February 16, 2026  ·  5 min read

✦

I woke up at 1am one night recently with a thought so clear it felt like someone had switched on a light in a very dark room. It was this: every harsh criticism I have ever felt from another person was not their voice I was carrying. It was mine.

I lay there and examined it from every angle, the way you do with a thought that feels important and slightly uncomfortable. My ego was resistant. It kept insisting: no, that's not right, these were real things that real people said to you. And yes, they were. But here is what I have slowly come to understand: a passing comment made in a moment stays only as long as we choose to carry it. The person who said it moved on. We did not.

The nose story

I grew up hearing that my nose was not quite right. Family members would make the occasional remark, the kind of offhand comment that adults make without thinking, and that children swallow whole. For years I believed them. I genuinely believed my nose was a problem that needed to be fixed. I looked into all the options. I even tried fillers once, and they made things worse because of course they did, you are adding volume to something you wanted to minimise. I eventually found my workaround: tilt the head slightly to the left in photographs, angle the face just so. I had a whole system.

And then one day I checked in with people who actually knew me, people who had no reason to manage my feelings, and they said: what are you talking about? You have a lovely nose. You always have.

The voice in my head had been so loud, so certain, so authoritative, that I had never once questioned whether it was telling the truth.

Words from others land on us in a moment and then they move on. We are the ones who pick them up, carry them home, and label them as fact.

What I want you to know

My darlings, you are going to hear things about yourselves throughout your lives. Some will be kind and some will not. Some will be said deliberately and most will be said without a second thought by people who will not even remember saying them a week later. And your mind, if you are not paying attention, will collect the unkind ones and file them under: things that are true about me.

This is not a character flaw. It is something that happens to almost everyone, especially when the words find us young, when we are still forming our ideas of who we are and what we are worth. The ego hears a critical word and decides to hold onto it as a warning, a protection, a thing to be vigilant about. What it does not understand is that in doing so, it is often protecting us from nothing real at all.

So here is what I would like to invite you to do, whenever you find yourself replaying a criticism that will not leave you alone: ask where it is really coming from. Not as a way to dismiss it entirely, because sometimes feedback is useful. But ask honestly: is this a universal truth, or is it a passing word from an unexamined moment, that I have been keeping alive all by myself?

And when you need a reference point for what is true about you, come here. Come back to this. Because from where I am standing, the two of you are the most extraordinary humans I have ever had the privilege of knowing. Every single day, without doing a single extraordinary thing, you are enough. You are more than enough. You always have been.

With love, always, Mum x
