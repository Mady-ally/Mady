Resourcefulness in Real Life (or: I Did My Own Rebonding Today)

April 1, 2026  ·  4 min read

✦

I have written before about resourcefulness as a concept, about choosing creativity over excuses and finding a way when the obvious way is blocked. Today I want to tell you about what that actually looks like on an ordinary Tuesday morning in Singapore, in practical, slightly unglamorous, very satisfying detail.

I did my own hair rebonding today.

Every six months or so I get my hair chemically straightened. In a salon this takes five hours in the seat and costs around $500. Today I did it at home with a Shiseido rebonding cream and neutralizer that cost me roughly $5 in total, and I was done in an hour and a half. My hair is straight. It looks exactly the same as it does when a professional does it. I am sitting here writing this feeling not one bit like a cheapskate and quite a lot like someone who figured something out.

Resourcefulness is not about being frugal. It is about refusing to accept that the expensive, time-consuming, conventional way is the only way.

Singapore is one of the most expensive cities in the world to live in, and I have always felt a little bit like it is a puzzle to be solved rather than a burden to be endured. Finding ways to live well here without handing over money for things I can do myself gives me a genuine thrill. It is like a game, and today I won a round.

The rest of the day was just as good

After the rebonding was done and my hair was hanging straight and I had done a small victory lap of the living room, we took Mason to the park. He is at the age where every outing is a full sensory event and the park did not disappoint. He met two new babies from the condo and a stay-at-home mum, and he did what Mason does with new people: observed carefully for a while and then, when he had decided they were acceptable, got down to the important business of playing. He had a proper workout. The kind that makes a two-year-old eat a good lunch and sleep without argument.

When we got home, Meimei woke up, and we decided today was the day for her first swim.

Meimei in the pool

I want to record this properly because first swims do not happen twice. We put her on a float and she sat there like she had been doing it all her life, completely unbothered, moving herself around the pool with a calm confidence that made everyone watching her laugh. She is not yet a year old. She has never been in a pool. She did not care. She just sat on her float and surveyed the situation and decided it was fine.

Mason, meanwhile, had his excavator toy and was so deeply happy he refused to get out when it was time. We had to negotiate. He drove a hard bargain. Eventually we reached an agreement involving the promise of food, which is how most negotiations with Mason end.

There is something about water that reaches children at a frequency adults cannot quite access. They come out of the pool heavy and soft and ready for sleep in a way that nothing else reliably produces. Both of mine were exhausted by lunchtime and I felt like I had discovered a cheat code.

A note about tonight

My husband plans two date nights for us every month. I want you to know this about your father: he is the kind of man who thinks about these things and then actually does them, not occasionally, but consistently, without being asked. Tonight we are going to dinner at Paul's, one of our favourite chefs in Singapore, and I am looking forward to it with the particular joy of someone who has had a very full and satisfying day and is now about to end it beautifully.

Some days just land perfectly. Today was one of them. Straight hair, two happy children in a pool, a park full of new friends, and a date with the person I love most. I want you to know that this is what a good life looks like to me. Not spectacular. Just full, and chosen, and mine.

With love, Mum x
