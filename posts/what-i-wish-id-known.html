What I Wish Someone Had Said to Me at Fifteen

March 12, 2026  ·  5 min read

✦

If I could sit next to my fifteen-year-old self for an hour, just one honest hour, there are a few things I would say. Not to change the path, because the path brought me here and I am grateful for every part of it. But because there is a particular kind of loneliness in not knowing the things that would have made the journey so much lighter.

I grew up in Singapore in neighbourhood schools, and by secondary school I already had the feeling that I did not quite fit the mould of the place. I did not know what I wanted to be. I only knew what I did not want. I kept my head down and found my way through and got to where I was going, but I did it the long way, by overriding most of what I instinctively knew and following what I thought was expected instead.

Your early preferences are data, not phases

Here is the thing nobody told me at fifteen, the thing I want you to have much earlier than I did: the things you enjoy, who you feel safe with, what drains you, what lights you up without explanation. These are not random. They are not phases to be grown out of. They are your nervous system responding before your intellect has learned to interfere. They are the earliest, most honest signals of who you actually are.

Instead of being taught to notice these signals, most of us are taught to override them. Fit in. Do not be difficult. Do not be different. And so we learn to perform a version of ourselves that is more acceptable, and we carry the exhaustion of that performance for years before we think to set it down.

Pay attention to what you enjoy. It is trying to tell you something true about who you are.

On your twenties: strengths, not identity

When you get to your twenties and everyone is asking what you want to do with your life, try to replace that question with a better one: what are you naturally good at, and what are you genuinely not? Not everyone is meant to lead. Not everyone is meant to be visible or competitive or relentlessly driven. And that is not failure. That is just design. Your energy leaks fastest when you build your life around what you think you should be good at. Your life stabilises when you stop forcing strengths you do not have.

On your thirties: divergence is lonely but right

Your thirties are when comparison peaks. Everyone around you will seem to have it figured out. Lives will look structured and choices will look intentional. Some of them will be. Many will just be a very convincing performance of stability. When you start to question the path that everyone seems to be walking, it will feel lonely. It will feel like you are getting something wrong. You are not. You are just becoming more honest about what you actually want, and that almost always happens quietly, ahead of schedule, and without anyone else's permission.

What never changes

Here is what I know to be evergreen, the things that will still be true no matter what the world looks like by the time you read this: your health, your energy, your self-respect, your autonomy, and your knowing of yourself. If something costs you any of these things, it is too expensive. No matter how impressive it looks from the outside.

And trust your instincts. They have been trying to help you since the very beginning. The sooner you let them, the easier everything becomes.

With so much love, Mum x
