Why I Left, and Why I Would Do It Again Tomorrow

March 4, 2026  ·  5 min read

✦

The morning after my last day at work, I made coffee with Mason. He is two years old and he is very serious about helping with things. He stood on his little step, watching the coffee go into the cup with the concentrated expression he saves for important tasks, and I thought: this is why.

I left a fifteen-year corporate career on 2nd March 2026. The lead-up to the last day was emotional and messy and full of the mixed feelings you'd expect from leaving something that had given me so much and cost me so much in equal measure. But the morning after? I woke up at peace. A peace so unfamiliar I barely recognised it.

The thing nobody tells you about time

A few months before I resigned, I came across a concept that I haven't been able to stop thinking about. If the average lifespan is around 76 years, that gives each of us roughly 4,000 weeks on this earth. Just 4,000. In a year there are 52. At 38 years old, I have approximately 1,800 weeks left. That is not very many. That number does not leave room for "I'll be more present when things settle down."

I sat with that number for a long time. I thought about all the ways I had been negotiating with the one thing in my life that was absolutely non-negotiable. We spend our lives treating time as if it is something we can earn more of. We borrow against it, trade it away, defer it. And then one day we realise we have been handing it over for things that were not worth it.

Money serves one purpose well: to provide. Its one other job, the most important one, is to buy you time. Not status. Not things. Time. The actual hours and days and weeks of your life. Everything else is noise.

I used fifteen years in corporate to buy myself thirty-three years of good life. I made peace with that trade. And when it was time to leave, I left. I did not wait for the next paycheck or the next milestone or the right moment. Because for every month I stayed past the point when I already knew, I was paying back ten times more in the only currency that cannot be refunded.

What I wanted to come home to

When I was on maternity leave with Mason, I had seven months at home with him. Then another seven with Meimei. Those months shaped me more than the previous fifteen years put together. I tasted what it was like to be fully present for the small, ordinary hours of the day, and I could not unfeel it.

I want to be home when Mason makes his new word connections in the morning. I want to be there when Meimei starts to figure out who she is. I want to read with them and cook with them and take them out in the Singapore sunshine and answer their questions and let them watch me decide, every day, that this life, this loud and beautiful and exhausting life, is exactly where I want to be.

I left corporate because of me. I want you both to know that. Not as a sacrifice laid at your feet, but as the most deliberate and joyful act of self-determination I have ever made. I knew what I wanted. I chose it with my whole heart.

Sometimes, life pushes you towards a certain direction. I have learnt not to resist. The peace I felt that first morning was not relief. It was recognition. This is where I was always supposed to be, doing the work that matters most.

With all my love, Mum x
